# How Myofunctional Therapy Improves Sleep Apnea

Myofunctional therapy has been shown in research to significantly improve obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) symptoms by addressing the underlying muscle dysfunction that contributes to airway collapse.

## Understanding Sleep Apnea

**Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA)** occurs when the airway repeatedly collapses during sleep, causing:
- Breathing pauses (apneas)
- Reduced oxygen levels
- Sleep fragmentation
- Daytime fatigue and health risks

The severity is measured by the Apnea-Hypopnea Index (AHI):
- **Mild:** 5-15 events per hour
- **Moderate:** 15-30 events per hour
- **Severe:** 30+ events per hour

## How Tongue Position Affects the Airway

During sleep, the tongue can fall back and obstruct the airway, especially when:

- The tongue is weak or has poor muscle tone
- The tongue rests low in the mouth (not on the palate)
- There is mouth breathing
- The airway space is already reduced

![Correct tongue resting position](/images/learn/correct-tongue-posture.jpg)

**Proper tongue posture** keeps the tongue forward and up, maintaining airway space even during sleep.

## How Myofunctional Therapy Helps

### Strengthens Tongue Muscles

Exercises target the critical muscles that support the airway:

- Genioglossus (main tongue muscle)
- Other tongue muscles
- Soft palate muscles

**Result:** A stronger tongue is less likely to collapse backward during sleep.

### Improves Tongue Position

Training the tongue to rest on the palate provides essential airway support:

- Keeps it forward and up
- Maintains airway space
- Prevents backward collapse

### Promotes Nasal Breathing

Nasal breathing naturally supports better sleep quality:

- Keeps the mouth closed during sleep
- Prevents the tongue from falling back
- Maintains better airway patency

### Strengthens the Soft Palate

A stronger, more toned soft palate reduces sleep disruption:

- Reduces snoring
- Decreases airway collapse
- Improves breathing during sleep

## Research Evidence

Multiple studies have shown that myofunctional therapy:

- **Reduces AHI by approximately 50%** in adults with moderate OSA
- **Decreases snoring frequency and intensity**
- **Improves oxygen saturation during sleep**
- **Reduces daytime sleepiness**
- **Improves quality of life**

One landmark study (Guimarães et al., 2009) found that myofunctional therapy reduced:
- AHI by 50%
- Snoring frequency by 36%
- Snoring intensity by 59%

## Who Benefits Most

Myofunctional therapy is most effective for:

- **Mild to moderate OSA** (though it can help severe cases as an adjunct)
- **Primary snoring** without significant apnea
- **UARS** (Upper Airway Resistance Syndrome)
- **Children** with sleep-disordered breathing
- **CPAP users** wanting to improve compliance or reduce pressure needs
- **Post-surgical patients** (after UPPP, MMA, etc.) to maintain results

## Combination with Other Treatments

Myofunctional therapy works well alongside:

- **CPAP therapy** - Can reduce pressure needs and improve compliance
- **Oral appliances** - Enhances effectiveness
- **Surgical treatments** - Helps maintain results and prevent relapse
- **Weight loss** - Addresses multiple contributing factors
- **Positional therapy** - Improves overall outcomes

Results vary by individual. Sleep study improvements are typically measured after completing the full program.

## Long-Term Success

For lasting results:

- Complete the full program
- Maintain awareness of tongue posture
- Continue nasal breathing
- Practice key exercises if needed for maintenance
- Address weight, allergies, or other contributing factors

## When to Seek Additional Treatment

Myofunctional therapy may not be enough as a standalone treatment if:

- OSA is severe (AHI > 30)
- Anatomical obstructions are significant
- Weight is a major factor
- Other medical conditions are present

In these cases, myofunctional therapy should be used **in combination with** CPAP, oral appliances, or surgery, not as a replacement.

## The Bottom Line

Myofunctional therapy addresses the muscular and functional causes of airway collapse, making it an effective treatment for many people with sleep-disordered breathing. It's non-invasive, has no side effects, and can produce lasting results when exercises are performed consistently.
